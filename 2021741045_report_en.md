## 1. Introduction

This project extends the existing Raccoonbot_Openvla pipeline — which fine-tunes the OpenVLA-7B model to control a 4-DOF RaccoonBot robot arm in a MuJoCo simulation environment — to a chess-piece pick-and-place task.

The original pipeline used simple colored cylinders as manipulation targets and performed only horizontal grasping. This project simultaneously satisfies the following three dataset-extension conditions:

- **New objects:** Rook and Bishop pieces on a 3×3 mini chessboard
- **New task:** Chess-rule-based piece movement and capture
- **New language commands:** Natural-language commands based on chess algebraic notation

In addition, two code improvements were implemented:

- **Improvement ④:** Pitch-aware (wrist-angle) inverse kinematics (IK)
- **Improvement ⑥:** Visualization — episode GIFs, success-rate graphs, place-error distributions

Development proceeded in an iterative cycle: camera repositioning → single-scenario data generation → VLA inference testing → failure-cause analysis → data augmentation → full-dataset collection → fine-tuning → evaluation.

## 2. Dataset Extension

### 2.1 New Objects — Chess Pieces and a Mini Chessboard

A 3×3 chessboard (usable cells: a2, b2, c2, a3, b3, c3; row 1 is excluded as unreachable) was built in MuJoCo XML, and two types of chess pieces were introduced as manipulation targets.

| Piece | Color | STL file | Height | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rook | White | chess_knight.stl | 60.6 mm | Requires top-down grasp |
| Bishop | Black | chess_rook.stl | 75 mm | Requires top-down grasp |

**Camera repositioning:** With the original front_view camera angle, front pieces occluded rear pieces, preventing the VLA model from accurately judging the board state. To resolve this, the camera was repositioned and angled closer to a top-view. By switching to a top-down viewpoint, all pieces were captured in the observation image without occlusion, allowing the VLA model to accurately recognize each piece's position.

### 2.2 New Task — Chess-Rule-Based Piece Movement

- **Rook:** Straight-line movement along the same row/column, no jumping, can capture opponent pieces
- **Bishop:** Diagonal movement (one cell on the 3×2 usable board), capture possible
- **Capture:** A 2-leg episode — first move the piece to be captured to the graveyard, then move the capturing piece to the destination

A total of 126 scenarios were classified into four categories:

| Category | Description | # Scenarios | Difficulty |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Cat 1 | Plain pick-and-place (no blocking, no capture) | 80 | Low |
| Cat 2 | Top-down pitch required (horizontal approach blocked) | 20 | Medium |
| Cat 3 | Capture → graveyard (no pitch) | 23 | Medium |
| Cat 4 | Capture → graveyard + pitch required | 3 | High |

### 2.3 New Language Commands — Based on Chess Notation

Each episode is conditioned on a natural-language command derived from chess algebraic notation:

> "Move the white rook from a2 to a3."
>
> "Move the black bishop from c3 to b2 to capture the white rook."

This format forces the VLA model to ground its language to specific board positions and is generated deterministically through the `scenario_instruction()` function.

## 3. Initial Experiments and Data Augmentation

### 3.1 Initial Dataset — Single Scenario

Initial data collection focused on a single scenario (advancing the white rook one cell from a2 to a3). A scenario in which the bishop advances one cell was added later, but in both cases VLA inference consistently failed to place the piece at the exact target position.

Failure-cause analysis:

- **Lack of visual diversity:** Deterministic single-scenario episodes are informationally equivalent. The model can ignore the image and output a fixed action.
- **No cross-scenario generalization:** Without diverse scenarios, there is no basis for instruction-conditioned behavior.

### 3.2 Data Augmentation Strategy

- **Spatial diversity:** Each piece is placed with a random offset from the cell center (truncated Gaussian, σ = cell_radius/2.5) and a random yaw (±30°)
- **Scenario coverage:** All 126 scenarios covering every legal rook and bishop move on the 3×2 usable grid
- **Language diversity:** Each scenario has a unique command string; capture scenarios include the name of the captured piece

Target collection: 8 successful episodes per scenario = about 1,008 total. Failed episodes (grasp failure, piece toppling, pushing another piece) are discarded and regenerated.

## 4. Code Improvement ④ — Pitch-Aware Inverse Kinematics (IK)

### 4.1 Motivation

The original `raccoon_env.py` fixes Joint 4 horizontally: `th4 = -(th2+th3) - 90°`. For chess pieces this causes two problems:

- **Blocked approach:** When another piece blocks horizontal approach, a top-down grasp is required
- **Bishop geometry:** The bishop's narrow tip (z ≈ 74–75 mm) requires a low-angle pitch approach

### 4.2 Implementation — raccoon_env_pitch_real.py

`PitchRealRaccoonEnv` inherits from `SyncSimRaccoonEnv` and adds the following:

Pitch-aware IK: offsets the wrist center by the pitch:

```
wx = x - L4 * cos(pitch) * c1,   z_eff = z + L4 * sin(pitch)
th4 = -(th2 + th3) - 90° - pitch
```

- **J4 joint limit:** rejects solutions with `|th4| > 105°` and tries the next candidate
- **lockp(pitch_deg):** maintains pitch throughout the entire lift/sweep/place process
- **set_motion_speed_scale(scale):** separate deceleration during the lift and place-down phases to reduce piece oscillation
- **Blocking detection:** uses a top-down pitch grid of [45°–50°] for the rook and [18°–22°] for the bishop

### 4.3 Action Encoding

A 7-dimensional action vector encodes pitch as an absolute angle:

```
action = [dx_m, dy_m, dz_m, 0, pitch_abs_deg, 0, gripper]
```

It is designed symmetrically with the data-generation encoding so that the VLA model learns to output the correct pitch for each scenario category.

## 5. Fine-Tuning

OpenVLA-7B was fine-tuned with LoRA (rank 32):

- **Trainable parameters:** about 1.45% (110M / 7.65B)
- **Training infrastructure:** A100 80GB GPU
- **Total training:** about 30,000 steps (about 12 hours)

```bash
torchrun vla-scripts/finetune.py \
  --vla_path openvla/openvla-7b \
  --dataset_name raccoon_pick_place \
  --lora_rank 32 --batch_size 8 --learning_rate 5e-4
```

## 6. Evaluation

### 6.1 Evaluation Setup

Using `chess_eval.py`, each scenario was run in the MuJoCo simulation with the fine-tuned OpenVLA-7B checkpoint.

**Hybrid policy:** Once the VLA model reaches the pick position, a script-based controller executes grasp → lift → sweep → place → release.

Success criteria:

- Place error < 1.8 cm
- Piece remains upright (|Δz| < 2.0 cm)
- Non-moving pieces undisturbed (displacement < 1.5 cm)

### 6.2 Results

The full set of 126 scenarios was rolled out 10 times each, for a total of 1,260 episodes.

| Category | Description | # Scenarios | Success/Total | Success Rate |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Cat 1 | Plain pick-and-place | 80 | 725 / 800 | 90.6% |
| Cat 2 | Pitch top-down | 20 | 149 / 200 | 74.5% |
| Cat 3 | Capture (no pitch) | 23 | 196 / 230 | 85.2% |
| Cat 4 | Capture + pitch | 3 | 20 / 30 | 66.7% |
| **Total** | | **126** | **1090 / 1260** | **86.5%** |

The overall success rate of 86.5% shows that the pipeline — with the dataset extension and the pitch-IK improvement applied — performs the complex chess task reliably. By category, plain pick-and-place (Cat 1, 90.6%) is highest, while cases that require pitch (Cat 2, 74.5%) or a 2-leg capture (Cat 3, 85.2%) show lower success rates in line with their difficulty. The hardest case, capture + pitch (Cat 4), is 66.7%, reflecting the difficulty of cases that simultaneously require multi-leg sequencing and a top-down grasp.

## 8. Conclusion

This project extended the existing Raccoonbot_Openvla pipeline — which had been limited to simple grasping of colored cylinders — to a pick-and-place domain with the structured constraints of chess rules. In doing so, it went beyond merely adding new objects: it simultaneously satisfied the three conditions of dataset extension (new objects, new task, new language commands) within a single coherent chess scenario.

The most central technical contribution is the pitch-aware inverse kinematics (Code Improvement ④). The original code fixed the wrist angle horizontally and could not handle blocked approaches or the bishop's narrow geometry, but by integrating pitch into the IK it enabled top-down grasping, which was essential for performing the Cat 2 and Cat 4 scenarios.

Another important lesson learned during development was that a model trained on only a single scenario exhibited the limitation of ignoring the image and repeating a fixed motion. This was resolved through spatial diversity (random offsets and yaw) and full coverage of all 126 scenarios, inducing the model to actually ground language commands to visual observations.

Quantitatively, a success rate of 86.5% was achieved across all 1,260 episodes, and a reasonable trend was confirmed in which the success rate decreases step by step as task difficulty increases (Cat 1 → Cat 4). This demonstrates that the dataset extension and code improvements work effectively across the entire VLA pipeline.

Future work could extend the currently limited two piece types (rook, bishop) to pawns, knights, queens, and kings, and scale the 3×3 mini board up to a full 8×8 chessboard. This would advance the system beyond simple pick-and-place toward one capable of playing actual chess games.
